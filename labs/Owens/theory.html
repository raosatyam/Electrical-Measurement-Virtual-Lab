Owen's Bridge

Theory

The theory of Owen’s bridge states that the unknown inductance `L_1` is compared with the known capacitor `C_4` connected to the arms of the bridge circuit. In the balanced condition, the non-inductive resistor `R_2` and the variable standard capacitor `C_2` can be varied independently. Hence, no current flows through the bridge circuit and no potential will be recorded by the null detector.

From Owen’s bridge circuit, we can observe:
- Unknown self-inductance `L_1`
- Pure resistor `R_3` (fixed non-inductive resistance)
- Fixed standard capacitor `C_4`
- Variable non-inductive resistor `R_2` in series with a variable standard capacitor `C_2`.
- A null detector is connected to determine the balance condition of the bridge circuit.

Consider the balanced equation of a basic AC bridge circuit:
`Z_1Z_4=Z_2Z_3`

Now, substitute the impedances of Owen’s bridge circuit in the above equation, then:
`((R_1+jωL_1)(1/jωC_4) = (R_2+1/jωC_2)R_3)`

Now, separate the real and imaginary terms from the above equation. We get:
`(L_1=R_2R_3C_4)`

The unknown inductance can be measured from the above equation:
`(R_1= R_3 left(frac(C_4)(C_2)right))`

The value of a variable standard capacitor `C_2` is measured.
